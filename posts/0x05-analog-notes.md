I keep a paper notebook. Field Notes, the boring kraft ones, three to a pack. I burn through one every six weeks or so. They live in a stack on the shelf above my desk, dated on the spine in silver Sharpie.

People ask me why. The honest answer is that I trust my hands more than I trust software.

### // What the notebook is for

Anything I want to actually remember. Architecture sketches. The shape of an exploit before I write the code. Phone numbers. Lock combinations I shouldn't be writing down. The first three lines of a poem that won't leave me alone.

Anything I want to think about slowly. Pen forces a rate limit. You can't autocomplete a thought in cursive.

### // What it isn't for

Anything searchable. Anything I need to share. Anything that benefits from being copy-pasted. For those I use plain text files in a flat folder, synced over Syncthing, edited in nvim. No app, no graph view, no AI assistant. The folder is the database.

The notebooks aren't precious. I'm not building a system. I'm just writing things down. When the current one fills up I start a new one, and the old one goes on the shelf, and I almost never open it again. That's fine. The point was the writing, not the reading.
