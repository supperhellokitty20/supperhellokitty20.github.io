Three years ago today I sent a calendar invite called 'quick chat' to my skip-level and resigned thirty minutes later. I had four months of runway and one signed contract. I felt indestructible. I was, in retrospect, mostly tired.

### // What I expected

More technical work. Bigger targets. The freedom to go deep on a single bug for weeks. A vague sense that I'd finally be doing the 'real' version of what I'd been hired to do at the corp.

### // What I got

Roughly forty percent of my time is invoicing, scoping, and email. Another thirty is writing reports nobody enjoys reading. Twenty is the actual work. The last ten is the weird overhead of being a one-person business — taxes, insurance, the dentist asking who my employer is.

The work itself is better. The targets are smaller but more interesting. I get to say no, which is the single biggest upgrade nobody talks about. I said no to a contract last month because I didn't like how the client talked about their users. That option didn't exist when I had a manager.

### // The honest part

I make less money. Not a lot less, but less. I work fewer hours but I think about work more. The line between weekend and weekday is gone and I haven't decided whether that's a feature.

Would I go back? No. But I won't pretend it's a Mr. Robot fantasy out here. Mostly it's me, this desk, the notebook on the shelf, and the next contract.
