I do this every couple years. Strip everything back to text. See what I actually need versus what I've accumulated. This time I went a step further and lived entirely inside a single tmux session for a week.

### // The setup

i3 as the WM but only one workspace, with a single full-screen alacritty. Inside it, tmux. Inside tmux, everything: nvim for code, w3m for the web, mutt for email, ncmpcpp for music, weechat for IRC, btop for the panic attacks.

### // What worked

Email got faster. Way faster. Mutt's keybinds are muscle memory after about two days and the lack of a unified inbox view forced me to actually triage. Code review in nvim with a diff buffer is better than any web UI. IRC has been good for thirty years and remains good.

### // What broke

Anything with a map. Anything with a video. The bank. Signal desktop, which I'd forgotten I depend on. PDFs with figures, which is most PDFs worth reading. A Zoom call I had to take from my phone in the kitchen.

Also: I missed images more than I expected. Not memes — diagrams. A good architecture diagram does in two seconds what three paragraphs of ASCII art can't.

### // What I kept

Mutt is back permanently. Nvim never left. I dropped my code editor's GUI for good. Everything else returned to its previous state and I'm okay with that. The point of these experiments isn't to live ascetically — it's to find out which tools I'd actually miss.
